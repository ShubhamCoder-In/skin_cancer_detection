Melanoma (mel)

Melanoma is the most dangerous type of skin cancer that develops from melanocytes, the pigment-producing cells of the skin. It can spread rapidly to other parts of the body if not detected early. It usually appears as a new spot on the skin or an existing mole that changes in color, size, or shape. Early detection is crucial for successful treatment. Learn more from the American Cancer Society.

Symptoms: A change in an existing mole, the development of a new, unusual growth, or a sore that doesn’t heal.

{% elif prediction == 'bkl' %}

Benign Keratosis-like Lesions (bkl)

Benign Keratosis-like Lesions are non-cancerous growths that resemble actinic keratoses but do not develop into cancer. They often appear as waxy, raised lesions that may look like they are "stuck on" the skin. Learn more from the Skin Cancer Foundation.

Symptoms: Waxy, raised lesions, often with a "stuck-on" appearance.

{% elif prediction == 'nv' %}

Nevus (nv)

Nevus, also known as moles, are benign growths on the skin that arise from melanocytes. They are typically harmless but should be monitored for any changes that might indicate melanoma. Learn more from the American Academy of Dermatology.

Symptoms: Regular moles are usually uniform in color and have a round or oval shape.

{% elif prediction == 'df, vasc, akiec, bcc' %}

Combined Category: df, vasc, akiec, bcc

This category includes a combination of Dermatofibroma (df), Vascular Lesions (vasc), Actinic Keratosis (akiec), and Basal Cell Carcinoma (bcc). Each type has its unique characteristics and implications.

Dermatofibroma (df): Dermatofibroma is a common benign skin nodule that is usually firm and has a brownish color. It is often found on the legs and can sometimes itch or be tender when touched. Learn more from Healthline.

Vascular Lesions (vasc): Vascular lesions are abnormalities of the blood vessels that appear on the skin. They can be red or purple and can vary in size and shape. While they are usually benign, they can sometimes cause cosmetic concerns or discomfort. Learn more from WebMD.

Actinic Keratosis (akiec): Actinic Keratosis is a precancerous growth that appears as a rough, scaly patch on sun-exposed areas. If left untreated, it can develop into squamous cell carcinoma. Learn more from the Mayo Clinic.

Basal Cell Carcinoma (bcc): Basal Cell Carcinoma is the most common type of skin cancer. It typically appears as a pearly or waxy bump, often with visible blood vessels. Although it rarely spreads to other parts of the body, it can cause significant damage to surrounding tissues if not treated. Learn more from the Skin Cancer Foundation.

Each of these conditions requires careful monitoring and management to prevent complications. Regular skin checks and consultations with a dermatologist are essential for maintaining skin health and catching any potential issues early.

Tips to Protect Against Skin Cancer

Protecting your skin from harmful UV rays is crucial in preventing skin cancer. Here are some tips:

  • Wear sunscreen with a high SPF.
  • Avoid sun exposure during peak hours (10 AM - 4 PM).
  • Wear protective clothing, hats, and sunglasses.
  • Avoid tanning beds.
  • Regularly check your skin for any changes and consult a dermatologist if you notice anything unusual.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What are the warning signs of skin cancer?

Look for new growths, spots, bumps, patches, or sores that don't heal. Changes in the appearance of a mole or a sore that doesn’t heal are also warning signs.

How is skin cancer diagnosed?

A dermatologist will examine your skin and may take a biopsy of any suspicious areas for laboratory analysis.

Is skin cancer treatable?

Yes, skin cancer is highly treatable when detected early. Treatments may include surgical removal, radiation therapy, and topical medications.
